## Executive Summary

This deliverable documents the current law, as of 2026-06-23, governing contracts for student transportation services (including school bus contracts) between public school districts and private contractors, for the ten AL–GA states (Alabama, Alaska, Arizona, Arkansas, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Florida, Georgia), across five fields: maximum contract term, rebid required at term-end, renewal mechanics, statutory citation, and district-level interpretation variance. Each state was researched independently as a single recorder-of-law task and then adversarially validated; every primary statutory citation was re-opened on its official source. No cross-state legal inference, ranking, or favorability judgment is recorded — only what the governing authority of each state says, anchored to the execution date.

## Notes / Special Provisions

### Alabama

Alabama — Notes & Special Provisions (Title 16, Chapter 13B governs the competitive bidding of city and county boards of education for pupil-transportation contracts):

- Dollar threshold / small-purchase exemption. Competitive sealed bidding is triggered at $40,000 (Ala. Code § 16-13B-1(a)(1), as amended by Act 2023-203). Expenditures below the threshold are not subject to the competitive-bidding mandate of Chapter 13B. The threshold is subject to CPI adjustment beginning Oct. 1, 2027 (§ 16-13B-1(a)(2)). A pupil-transportation contract is virtually certain to exceed this threshold in any LEA of meaningful size.
- Sole-source procurement. Permitted under § 16-13B-7(b) where the awarding authority makes a written determination, supported by specifications, that only one source is available; sole-source designation is narrowly defined and subject to documentation requirements.
- Emergency procurement. § 16-13B-3 permits letting a contract without public advertisement "to the extent necessary to meet the emergency" where the awarding authority declares an emergency in writing affecting public health, safety, or convenience. This is a fact-specific exception, not a routine path to renewal of a pupil-transportation contract.
- Cooperative purchasing / piggyback authority. § 16-13B-2(a)(13) permits purchases through national or regional cooperative purchasing programs approved by the Department of Examiners of Public Accounts. § 16-13B-2(a)(15) permits purchases through GSA vendors. Cooperative-purchasing pathways operate as procurement vehicles for new contracts, not as a path to renew an expired contract without a fresh competitive process.
- Child Nutrition Program emergency exception. § 16-13B-2.1 (Act 2022-264) permits an emergency-driven exception for goods or services related to the Child Nutrition Program, limited to 60 days and requiring State Superintendent recommendation and Department of Examiners of Public Accounts approval. Does not reach pupil transportation.
- Second-lowest-responsible-bidder substitution. Under § 16-13B-7(a), upon documented default of the awarded bidder during the contract term, the awarding authority may substitute the second-lowest responsible bidder "for the remainder of the award period without rebidding." This operates within an existing award only; it is not a renewal-without-rebid mechanism at term-end.
- Renewal-without-rebid exception (limited). § 16-13B-2(a)(9) is the only "existing contracts up for renewal" exception and is expressly limited to "sanitation or solid waste collection, recycling, or disposal." AG opinions construing this exception (e.g., AG 2015 to Calhoun County; AG 93-00310 DeKalb County; AG 93-00287 Winston County landfill; AG 94-00183 East Central Alabama Solid Waste Disposal Authority) are all anchored to the solid-waste carve-out and do not extend to pupil transportation.
- Public-works exclusion. Title 16, Chapter 13B expressly excludes contracts for public works (governed instead by Title 39). Pupil-transportation service contracts are not public works.
- Special-education / IEP / McKinney-Vento / foster-care / alternative transportation. No statutory or regulatory differentiation in Alabama. The general 5-year cap and competitive-bidding mandate apply equally to a privately-contracted special-education or alternative-route transportation contract that meets the $40,000 threshold. Federal IDEA, Section 504, and McKinney-Vento create the duty to provide transportation as a related service at no cost to the student/family but do not displace state contracting/procurement rules.
- Charter schools. Alabama public charter schools are authorized under Title 16, Chapter 6F. Whether a particular charter school is subject to Chapter 13B will turn on its authorizer relationship and charter contract terms.
- State-approval requirements. The Department of Examiners of Public Accounts approves cooperative purchasing programs under § 16-13B-2(a)(13). The State Superintendent must recommend any Child Nutrition Program emergency exception under § 16-13B-2.1. Otherwise no state pre-approval is required for a routine pupil-transportation contract award.
- Administrative regulation (Ala. Admin. Code ch. 290-2-4). Governs school-bus safety, specifications, used-equipment purchases, driver certification, transportation supervisors, mechanic certification, and accident reports. Contains no contract-term, rebid, or renewal provision for private contractors.
- Title 41, Chapter 16, Article 3 (general state/local bid law). By its terms reaches the Alabama Fire College, "the district boards of education of independent school districts," the county commissions, the governing bodies of the municipalities, and instrumentalities of counties and municipalities (3-year cap on services in § 41-16-57(f); $30,000 threshold in § 41-16-50(a)). Does NOT reach Alabama's modern city and county boards of education, which were expressly removed from Title 41 by Act 2009-760 and placed under Title 16, Chapter 13B.
- Title 16, Chapter 27 and Chapter 27A. Chapter 27 (Transportation of Pupils) covers school-bus safety, inspections, driver licensing, insurance, seat belts, and reporting. § 16-27-6 references "every contract between a board of education and a school bus contract operator" only for a required seat-belt clause. Chapter 27A (Alabama School Bus Safety Act) authorizes automated stop-arm camera enforcement. Neither chapter contains contract-term, rebid, or renewal provisions.

### Alaska

Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions: 4 AAC 27.086 contains no dollar-threshold or small-purchase exemption from the competitive-proposal-process mandate for pupil-transportation contracts. AS 36.30 small-purchase thresholds do not apply because AS 36.30 does not govern school-district pupil-transportation procurement: AS 14.08.101(3) expressly exempts regional school boards (REAAs) from AS 36.30, and AS 14.14.060(h) authorizes borough school boards to set their own purchasing policy separate from the borough — but 4 AAC 27.086 by its terms applies to all "district school boards" (4 AAC 27.990(11) defines "school district" to include borough and city school districts and REAAs), so the competitive-proposal mandate applies uniformly. Sole-source / emergency procurement: 4 AAC 27.086 contains no sole-source or emergency-procurement carve-out for pupil-transportation contracts. Cooperative-purchasing / piggyback authority: No statutory or regulatory pupil-transportation-specific cooperative-purchasing or piggyback authority is established in 4 AAC 27. Charter-school rules: Charter schools operate under contracts authorized by their local school district (AS 14.03.250-.290); pupil transportation provided to charter-school students remains within the district's pupil-transportation system and is therefore subject to 4 AAC 27.086 when procured via contract. In-term price-adjustment mechanics: Within the awarded contract term, the RFP must include fuel adjustments (4 AAC 27.086(a)(7)) and may include itemized activity-bus services and optional-equipment pricing (4 AAC 27.086(b)(2)-(3)). RFP scheduling sequence: RFP commencement 6 months before current contract's expiration; pre-bid conference 30 days after RFP release; proposals due 14 days after pre-bid conference (4 AAC 27.086(c)).

### Arkansas

**Arkansas — Notes / Special Provisions**

- **Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions (commodities only).** Ark. Code Ann. § 6-21-304(a)(1)(A)(i)(a) requires school districts to solicit bids only for "commodities" at or above $20,000 (annually CPI-adjusted on July 1 by the Commissioner of Elementary and Secondary Education under § 6-21-304(a)(1)(B)). Because § 6-21-301(1) defines "Commodities" to exclude "personal and professional services," the threshold does not apply to transportation services contracts.
- **Sole-source / emergency procurement (commodities only).** Ark. Code Ann. § 6-21-305 exempts from school-district bidding requirements: emergency commodities, commodities from the United States Government, utility services, used equipment and machinery (except used school buses), and single-source commodities. The exemption list operates entirely within "commodities" and does not extend the bidding regime to transportation services.
- **Used school bus purchases.** Ark. Code Ann. § 6-21-306 provides that "used school buses purchased by public school districts in this state shall not be subject to the jurisdiction of the State Procurement Director nor any competitive bidding procedures prescribed by law." This addresses the purchase of buses, not contracts with private transportation contractors.
- **Cooperative purchasing / piggyback authority.** Ark. Code Ann. § 19-11-249 authorizes (does not require) a "public procurement unit" to "participate in, sponsor, conduct, or administer a cooperative purchasing agreement." The authority is permissive, and § 19-11-206 defines the relevant terms. The Title 25 Ch. 20 Interlocal Cooperation Act likewise authorizes the permissive joint exercise of any power. Neither imposes a term, rebid, or renewal command on a district's own private transportation services contract.
- **Charter-school rules.** No separate Arkansas statute or DESE rule was located that imposes different contract term, rebid, or renewal mechanics on open-enrollment public charter schools' contracts with private student-transportation contractors. Charter-school transportation duties and operations are governed by the general charter-school framework and the same statutes (or statutory silence) applicable to traditional districts under §§ 6-19-101 and 6-19-102.
- **Special-education differences.** None located in Arkansas law. The DESE Office of Special Education IDEA Part B Fiscal Accountability Procedures Manual (updated 10/31/2022) addresses special-education transportation only as an IDEA Part B "related service" and fiscal allowable cost (e.g., "Allowable Special Education transportation costs include repair or servicing of Special Education vehicles, insurance, mileage, and bus driver and bus aide costs"; vehicles purchased with IDEA Part B funds "may be used only for Special Education-related activities"). It does not set state contract term, rebid, or renewal rules different from the general silence applicable to regular-route contracts. Federal IDEA (34 C.F.R. § 300.34(c)(16)) and McKinney-Vento (42 U.S.C. § 11432) govern the duty to provide transportation, not state contract/procurement rules.
- **State-approval requirements.** Ark. Code Ann. § 6-19-102(d) requires consent of the Division of Elementary and Secondary Education before a bus used in one district transports pupils in another, "except as specifically allowed by law." Bus specifications are prescribed by uniform rules of the Commission for Arkansas Public School Academic Facilities and Transportation under § 6-19-102(e) and § 6-19-101. No state-approval requirement was located for entering into, renewing, or extending a district-to-private-contractor pupil transportation services contract.
- **Board-member conflict prohibition.** Ark. Code Ann. § 6-19-102(c) provides that "Any contract with any member of the school district board of directors for the transportation of children or to drive a bus shall be null and void." This is a self-dealing prohibition and does not address term, rebid, or renewal of contracts with arm's-length private contractors.
- **Funding overlay.** The LEARNS Act (Act 2023, No. 237) Transportation Modernization Grant Program (codified within Title 6 and implemented by Commission rule at 6 CAR pt. 337, eff. 1/7/2025) provides grant funding to support pupil transportation but does not prescribe term, rebid, or renewal mechanics for district-to-private-contractor contracts.

### California

California — Notes / Special Provisions.

(1) Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemption. Cal. Educ. Code §39802 imposes competitive bidding (pursuant to PCC §§20111-20112) only when an expenditure on a transportation contract with a non-exempt party "exceeds ten thousand dollars ($10,000)." Transportation contracts at or below $10,000 are not subject to §39802 mandatory bidding. The $10,000 figure is a fixed statutory threshold and is NOT adjusted by the CDE annual inflation index applicable to PCC §20111's general services threshold (which is $119,100 effective Jan. 1, 2026). (Cal. Educ. Code §39802.)

(2) Statutory-party exemptions from §39802 bidding. Even above the $10,000 threshold, §39802 does not require competitive bidding when the contract is with (i) a common carrier, (ii) a municipally owned transit system (defined in §39800(b) as a transit system owned by a city or a district created under Pub. Util. Code §24501 et seq.), or (iii) a parent or guardian of the pupil being transported. (Cal. Educ. Code §§39800(b), 39802.)

(3) Award to other than the lowest bidder. Under §39802 the governing board "may let the contract for the service to other than the lowest bidder" — a transportation-specific deviation from the general PCC §20111(b) lowest-responsible-bidder rule that otherwise governs school-district services contracts.

(4) Cooperative-purchasing / piggyback authority. Cal. Pub. Contract Code §20118 authorizes school-district governing boards to participate in cooperative/piggyback purchases of "materials, supplies, equipment, automotive vehicles, tractors, and other personal property" without separate advertising, but the statute is limited to personal property and does not authorize a piggyback alternative for transportation SERVICES contracts. Piggyback authority does therefore not extend or override the §39803 5-year services cap.

(5) Sole-source / emergency procurement. No general sole-source or emergency-procurement carve-out specific to pupil-transportation services contracts was located in Educ. Code Part 23.5 or PCC §§20110-20118. Boards rely on §39802's statutory-party exemptions (common carrier, municipal transit, parent/guardian) and the under-$10,000 threshold for non-bid procurement.

(6) Charter-school rules. Charter schools are included in the §39803.5(f) definition of "local educational agency" for purposes of the zero-emission carve-out (15-year services / 15-or-20-year lease cap). The general §39803 (5-year services / 5-or-10-year lease) framework is written for "school districts" and "governing boards"; charter schools' procurement practices are otherwise governed by their charter, Educ. Code §47600 et seq., and applicable PCC provisions. No on-point statutory provision setting a different term cap or rebid rule for charter-school pupil-transportation contracts under §39803 (non-zero-emission) was located.

(7) Schoolbus lease / lease-purchase compliance. Per §39803(d), all schoolbus rentals, leases, and lease-purchase arrangements must comply with Article 3 (commencing with §17450) of Chapter 4, Part 10.5 (lease/lease-purchase of equipment or service systems). §17450 et seq. provides procedural compliance for the lease form but does not alter the 5/10-year cap in §39803(b) (or the 15/20-year cap in §39803.5(c) for zero-emission buses).

(8) In-term annual renegotiation. §39803(c) (and §39803.5(d) for zero-emission) authorizes annual price renegotiation within the contract period "when economic factors indicate negotiation is necessary to maintain an equitable pricing structure," subject to mutual approval — this is distinct from term-end renewal and does not extend the contract beyond the statutory cap.

(9) Special-education / IEP-mandated transportation. No different procurement statute, bid threshold, contract-term cap, or renewal mechanism applies to special-education, IEP-mandated, McKinney-Vento, foster-care, or alternative student-transportation vendor contracts. The §39802 / §39803 / §39803.5 framework applies uniformly. §39807.5 prohibits charging transportation fees to pupils with disabilities whose IEP includes transportation as a related service — a parent-fee restriction, not a procurement-mechanics differentiation. Federal IDEA (20 U.S.C. §1401(26); 34 C.F.R. §300.34) and McKinney-Vento (42 U.S.C. §11432) govern the LEA's duty to transport, not vendor-contract mechanics.

(10) State-approval requirements. No State Board of Education, CDE, State Superintendent, or other state-agency approval is required for entry into, renewal of, or renegotiation of a §39803 / §39803.5 pupil-transportation contract. Approval is by the local governing board and the contracting party.

(11) Driver-safety statute (SB 88 / §39875). Cal. Educ. Code §39875 (added by Stats. 2023) imposes driver-safety requirements on persons providing "school-related pupil transportation for compensation," with exemptions for parents/guardians and certain government employees. It does not modify §§39800-39809.5 contract-term, bidding, or renewal mechanics.

### Colorado

Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions. Colorado has no statewide small-purchase threshold applicable to school districts. CRS 22-32-109(1)(b) requires each board of education to adopt its own competitive-bidding procedures for goods and services other than professional services; the dollar threshold above which bids are required is set in the local policy (CASB Sample Policy DJE leaves this as a local fill-in).

Sole-source and emergency procurement. The state Procurement Code's sole-source (CRS 24-103-205) and emergency (CRS 24-103-206) provisions do not apply of their own force to school districts (CRS 24-101-105(1)(a),(2)). Any sole-source or emergency procurement authority for a district pupil-transportation contract derives from the district's locally adopted bidding/procurement policy under CRS 22-32-109(1)(b).

Cooperative purchasing / piggyback authority. Colorado school districts have express authority to act jointly. CRS 22-32-122(5)-(7) authorize boards to associate or contract with one or more other districts to provide services jointly. The Intergovernmental Relationships statutes (CRS 29-1-203) and the Procurement Code's cooperative-purchasing provisions (CRS 24-110-201 et seq.) are available, the latter only to the extent a district voluntarily adopts the Procurement Code (CRS 24-101-105(2)). No statute requires a transportation contract obtained via cooperative purchasing to be re-bid at term-end; mechanics remain set by the local policy and the cooperative agreement.

Charter-school rules. The Charter Schools Act (CRS 22-30.5-101 et seq.) treats a charter school as a public school of its authorizing district; charter contracts and transportation arrangements are governed by the charter contract with the authorizer. Institute charter schools (CRS 22-30.5-501 et seq.) contract with the Charter School Institute. No special statutory term, rebid, or renewal mechanic for charter-school transportation contracts with private contractors was located beyond the same CRS 22-32-110(1)(w)/22-32-122 + local-policy framework (and, for Institute schools, the Institute's own procurement policies).

Special-education differences. None at the contract/procurement level. CRS 22-20-101 et seq. (ECEA) and 1 CCR 301-8 implement the FAPE/IDEA duty (incorporating 34 CFR 300.107 and 300.139) but do not create separate term, rebid, or renewal rules for SPED transportation contracts. Procurement proceeds under CRS 22-32-110(1)(w) and 22-32-122 and the local CRS 22-32-109(1)(b) policy. Federal McKinney-Vento (42 U.S.C. § 11432) creates the duty to transport eligible homeless students but does not displace state contract or procurement rules.

State-approval requirements. No state-approval requirement attaches to ordinary multi-year pupil-transportation service contracts. CRS 22-32-127 (installment-purchase / lease-with-option indebtedness rules — including election or hearing requirements) is limited to real property and equipment and does not reach ordinary service contracts. Vehicle, operator, and inspection compliance is overseen by CDE under 1 CCR 301-25 and 1 CCR 301-26 but those rules do not regulate the contract's term, rebid, or renewal mechanics.

Cross-cutting contract terms. CRS 22-1-135 governs all public school contracts: (2)(d)(I) requires (and deems included) the non-appropriation clause, so every multi-year pupil-transportation contract is functionally subject to annual fiscal-year termination by operation of law; (2)(a) voids indemnification of the contractor by the district, binding-arbitration clauses, contractor-liability caps, Colorado Governmental Immunity Act waivers, and waivers of student-data-privacy protections. CRS 22-32-122(4) requires criminal background checks for any contractor providing direct services to students, including transportation.

### Connecticut

Connecticut — Notes / Special Provisions.

Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions. Connecticut imposes no statewide small-purchase or dollar-threshold framework for school-district pupil-transportation procurement. Where a municipality has adopted a competitive-bidding ordinance under CGS Sec. 7-148v, that ordinance may exempt contracts below an ordinance-set threshold (capped at $25,000 for sealed bidding). Such thresholds are local, not statewide, and Sec. 7-148v does not by its terms reach local or regional boards of education unless adopted into the municipal charter or applied through a local board procurement policy. (CGS Sec. 7-148v.)

Sole-source / emergency procurement. No state statute establishes sole-source or emergency-procurement procedures specifically for school-district pupil-transportation contracts. Local boards operating under a municipal competitive-bidding ordinance may have local sole-source/emergency procedures; the state competitive-bidding statutes (Title 4a, Chapter 58) apply only to state-agency purchases and not to local boards of education.

Cooperative purchasing / piggyback authority. Connecticut allows but does not require cooperative purchasing arrangements; the Capitol Region Education Council (CREC) operates a cooperative purchasing program available to school districts. Cooperative purchasing is an option, not a mandate, and does not displace the CGS Sec. 10-220(a) term cap when a board enters a transportation contract through such a vehicle.

Charter-school rules. Connecticut charter schools are governed by CGS Sec. 10-66aa et seq. No charter-school-specific transportation-contract term, rebid, or renewal rule was located; the host district's transportation duty under CGS Sec. 10-220 and Chapter 172 generally controls.

Special-education differences. As detailed in the Special Education field: no different statutory contract-term, rebid, or renewal rule applies. CGS Sec. 10-76d(e) requires curb-to-curb transportation for special-education students; Conn. Agencies Regs. Sec. 10-76d-19 imposes operational standards (one-hour travel cap absent written parental consent and SBE approval; in-service training; properly equipped vehicles; transportation aides; parent-transport reimbursement). 2025 Conn. Pub. Acts No. 25-67, Sec. 4 directs SDE to develop special-education outplacement transportation billing standards by January 1, 2027 (with which outplacement transportation costs must align beginning with the 2027-28 school year). 2025 Conn. Pub. Acts No. 25-93, Sec. 23 directs SDE to make a model special-education outplacement transportation contract available to boards by July 1, 2026 (permissive, non-binding template).

State-approval requirements. No state-approval requirement applies to the formation, renewal, or rebid of a pupil-transportation contract under CGS Sec. 10-220(a). Special-education programs and placements are subject to State Board of Education oversight under CGS Sec. 10-76 et seq.; the one-hour travel-time cap under Conn. Agencies Regs. Sec. 10-76d-19 can be exceeded only with written parental consent and SBE approval, but this is an operational rule, not a procurement approval.

Other rules affecting practical application. (i) Because Sec. 10-220(a) caps each contract at 5 (or 10) years and contains no renewal mechanic, at term-end a new contract is required; the procurement method (competitive RFP, negotiated, cooperative-purchasing piggyback) is a local choice unless local charter/ordinance directs otherwise. (ii) The 10-year option requires inclusion of at least one zero-emission school bus as defined in 42 USC Sec. 16091(a)(8); statutory text does not specify the minimum proportion, route assignment, or duration over which the zero-emission bus must be in service, and no SDE guidance or regulation construing the 10-year option was located. (iii) CGS Sec. 10-287(b) imposes a lowest-responsible-bidder mandate but applies only to school-construction projects and does not reach pupil-transportation services.

### Delaware

**State-approval requirement.** Every Delaware school-district pupil-transportation contract award and contract transfer requires dual approval by the local board of education and the Delaware Department of Education (14 Del. C. § 2901(b)(3), § 2908(c)). The DOE Transportation Office centrally collects the annual letter-of-commitment on or before May 1 each year (§ 2908(d)(4)).

**Procurement-chapter exemption.** Contracts for the transportation of school children are expressly exempted from the entire state procurement regime of Title 29 Chapter 69 by 29 Del. C. § 6904(c); the only governing authority is Title 14 Chapter 29 plus 14 Del. Admin. Code Reg. 1150. Because of this clean exemption, no general small-purchase or dollar-threshold exemption from Chapter 69 applies — the entire chapter is inapplicable.

**Sole-source / emergency procurement.** No sole-source or emergency-procurement provision specifically applicable to pupil-transportation contracts is codified in Title 14 Ch. 29. The functional equivalent is 14 Del. C. § 2908(b), which requires the responsible department or agency to furnish transportation directly if no replacement contractor can be found at contract expiration.

**Cooperative purchasing / piggyback.** Because Title 29 Ch. 69 (which houses Delaware's cooperative-purchasing authority) does not apply to school-transportation contracts under § 6904(c), there is no statutorily prescribed piggyback or interlocal mechanism specific to these contracts. Districts may collaborate on bid solicitations, and § 508(b)(1) expressly contemplates that charter schools may publicly bid transportation routes "either alone or with other local education agencies."

**Charter-school rules.** 14 Del. C. § 508(b) is the only Delaware provision expressly authorizing multi-year transportation contracts (for "1 or more years"), fuel-adjustment provisions (charter school bears increased payments), and multi-year funding mechanics (state funds guarantee year 1; subsequent years a local commitment). These authorities are limited by their terms to charter schools and do not extend to regular school districts.

**Special-education differences.** No separate contract-term, rebid, procurement, or renewal rules apply to special-education, IEP, McKinney-Vento, foster-care, or alternative-program transportation. 14 Del. C. § 2906 is funding-only; 14 Del. Admin. Code § 1150-23.0 addresses eligibility, IDEA financial-aid scope, residential-program specifics, and IEP-required state-funded bus aides — but contains no contracting mechanics differentiating special-ed transportation from regular routes. The § 2908 renewal/letter-of-commitment regime and the § 6904(c) procurement exemption apply uniformly to all "contracts for the transportation of school children."

**Right-of-first-refusal and bus-purchase obligations affecting practical application.** Under § 2908(a), the incumbent contractor has a statutory right of first refusal at every contract expiration not driven by bus-age limits. Under § 2908(c)(4), where a new contractor is selected via § 2908(a) advertising and the existing bus is less than 7 years old and still on the depreciation schedule, the new contractor must offer to purchase the existing bus at least at fair market value. These provisions materially affect entry, exit, and successor-contractor dynamics within the statutory framework.

**Inflation-factor escape clause.** Under § 2908(d)(1), the contractor's annual letter of commitment is not binding if the final budget appropriation act's inflation factor for the school transportation formula is less than the inflation factor in the Governor's proposed budget — a statutory escape valve tied to the annual budget cycle that operates in lieu of a CPI/index escalator.

### Florida

Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions. Fla. Admin. Code R. 6A-1.012(7) sets a hard-coded $50,000 threshold above which school districts must request competitive solicitations from three or more sources; the rule expressly prohibits dividing a procurement to avoid the threshold. Authorized commodities or contractual services at or below $50,000 may be procured without formal competitive solicitation under district policy.

Sole-source and emergency procurement. Rule 6A-1.012 authorizes sole-source procurement when the commodity or service is available from a single source, subject to a 7-business-day public-notice/posting requirement and written justification. Emergency procurement is permitted where the superintendent makes a written finding that an immediate danger to public health, safety, or welfare exists or that a substantial loss would result; pricing from at least two vendors must be obtained when practicable. These are general procurement exceptions, not transportation-specific carve-outs.

Cooperative-purchasing / piggyback authority. Rule 6A-1.012 authorizes school districts to purchase under contracts awarded by other governmental agencies, school boards, or state university cooperative agreements at the same terms and prices, in lieu of conducting their own competitive solicitation. Districts also have permissive access to state-agency cooperative-purchasing pathways under § 287.056, Fla. Stat. (as "governmental entities" under § 287.012). Districts may use piggyback authority to extend service continuity through other agencies' contracts rather than through formal renewals.

Reciprocal inter-district transportation. § 1006.21(5), Fla. Stat. requires contiguous school districts to provide for reciprocal policies and agreements for contracts for school-bus transportation services, inspections, and screening for public schools and public charter schools. This authorizes inter-district reciprocity for service but does not waive procurement requirements.

Bus pooling and district purchase-and-resale. § 1006.27, Fla. Stat. authorizes the Department of Education to administer a voluntary plan under which districts may pool bids for school buses and related supplies, and authorizes a district to purchase buses through the department and resell them to the contractor as part of the service contract. Sets no term cap or renewal mechanism.

Charter schools. § 1006.21(5), Fla. Stat. extends the contiguous-district reciprocity requirement to public charter schools. Florida charter-school statutes do not impose a separate procurement framework for transportation contracts beyond Rule 6A-1.012.

Special-education differences. No separate Florida statutory or SBE-rule contracting regime for special-education, IEP-mandated, McKinney-Vento, foster-care, or alternative-student transportation contracts. § 1006.22(1)(a), Fla. Stat. authorizes motor vehicles other than school buses "when the transportation is for physically handicapped or isolated students" and for "comprehensive contract[s] for a specialized educational program" between a district school board and a service provider who provides instruction, transportation, and other services (e.g., autism centers, DJJ facilities). § 1006.22(6) authorizes contracts with common carriers for athletic contests and school functions. Neither provision modifies the underlying procurement rule or establishes special-ed-specific term or renewal mechanics. FLDOE Technical Assistance Paper FY 2001-13 and FLDOE ESE guidance confirm IEP teams determine whether transportation is a related service for a particular student. Federal law (IDEA, McKinney-Vento) creates duties to transport but does not establish state contract or procurement rules.

State-approval requirements. No state-approval requirement is established by Florida statute or SBE rule for district school-board student-transportation contracts beyond the SBE-rule procurement framework in Rule 6A-1.012 and the bus-related provisions in § 1006.27 and Rule 6A-3.0171. Rule 6A-3.0171 (last amended Dec. 21, 2025) directs that contracts for operation of school buses and alternate vehicles comply with law and SBE rules; the 2025 amendment updated the Florida School Bus Safety Inspection Manual and inspection frequency (quarterly, max 101 calendar days) but did not introduce contract-procurement provisions.

Chapter 287 applicability. The 6-month extension cap in § 287.057(13) and the 3-year-or-original-term renewal cap in § 287.057(14), Fla. Stat. govern "agencies" as defined in § 287.012 (state executive-branch units only) and do not by their terms bind district school boards. School districts procure under Rule 6A-1.012 via § 1010.04(4)(a), Fla. Stat.

### Georgia

Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions. Georgia has no statewide school-district small-purchase or dollar-threshold rule that supplants competitive bidding for pupil-transportation services contracts, because there is no statewide competitive-bidding mandate for those service contracts in the first place. O.C.G.A. § 20-2-500 imposes only an in-state-vendor preference for "supplies, materials, equipment, or agricultural products" over $100,000 (including the purchase of school buses themselves) and does not reach services. O.C.G.A. § 50-5-69 (Georgia Procurement Registry) is a state-agency reporting regime and does not bind local boards of education.

Sole-source and emergency procurement. No state-law sole-source or emergency-procurement carve-out specific to pupil-transportation services contracts was located. Because the underlying procurement-method choice is left to local boards (Rule 160-5-3-.02 is silent on procurement method), sole-source and emergency-procurement practices are governed by local procurement policy.

Cooperative purchasing / piggyback authority. Title 50 Ch. 5 Pt. 2 (O.C.G.A. §§ 50-5-100 to 50-5-103) authorizes opt-in cooperative purchasing through the Department of Administrative Services (DOAS). Local boards of education may, but are not required to, use DOAS statewide contracts. No piggyback authority is mandated for pupil-transportation services.

Charter-school rules. State Charter Schools Commission "Contracting Best Practices" guidance offers generic procurement guidance for state-chartered schools but does not impose distinct term/rebid/renewal rules for pupil-transportation contracts beyond what § 20-2-504 / § 20-2-506 and Rule 160-5-3-.02 already establish.

Special-education differences. None. O.C.G.A. § 20-2-152(b) authorizes local school systems to provide special-education programs by contracting with public or private institutions; O.C.G.A. § 20-2-188(g) directs the SBOE to adopt policies and regulations for transportation of students with special needs and for payment of those transportation costs. Rule 160-5-3-.02 (Contracted Transportation Services) and Rule 160-5-3-.17 (Use of Alternative Vehicles, commonly used for special-education and homeless-student transport) prescribe vehicle/driver/insurance/background-check requirements only — not term, rebid, or renewal mechanics. The § 20-2-504 / § 20-2-506 framework therefore applies uniformly to regular-route and special-needs transportation contracts.

State-approval requirements. None for term-end renewal of pupil-transportation services contracts. § 20-2-506 contains no state-agency (GaDOE or otherwise) approval condition for execution or renewal of a multiyear contract for transportation services. The State Board's role under § 20-2-188(g) and Rule 160-5-3-.02 is regulatory (vehicle/driver/insurance/background-check standards), not procurement-approval.

Other rules affecting practical application of the five fields. (i) § 20-2-506(b)(4)'s 7.5% local-M&O-revenue cap on aggregate annual payments under § 20-2-506 plus Art. IX, Sec. III, Para. I intergovernmental contracts (excluding guaranteed energy savings contracts and contracts with other public educational entities for the education of students) functionally constrains the dollar size of multiyear transportation contracts in a given calendar year. (ii) § 20-2-506(c)(1)'s optional fiscal-funding-out clause allows non-appropriation termination. (iii) § 20-2-506(d)–(e) limit the contract obligation to the calendar year of execution (or any renewal year), so no multi-year debt is created. (iv) § 20-2-506(g) bars a school system, for four calendar years following a defeated bond referendum, from entering any multiyear contract for the lease, purchase, or lease purchase of items substantially similar to those proposed for the defeated bonded debt. (v) Rule 160-5-3-.02 requires LEAs, before entering any pupil-transportation agreement, to verify the carrier's RCC/DPS certification and obtain proof of legally required minimum insurance, and to require DPS/GCIC driver background checks.
