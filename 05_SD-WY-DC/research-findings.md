# Student Transportation Contract Rules — Set 5 (SD–WY + D.C.): Research Findings

*Current law as of 2026-06-24. Neutral recorder of law; no favorability judgments; each jurisdiction researched independently and adversarially validated; no cross-jurisdiction legal inference.*

## Executive Summary

This document records the current law, as of 2026-06-24, governing contracts for student transportation services (including school bus contracts) between public school districts (and, for the District of Columbia, DCPS and the public charter LEAs) and private contractors, for the eleven jurisdictions District of Columbia, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin, and Wyoming, across five fields: maximum contract term, whether a competitive rebid is required at term-end, renewal mechanics, the controlling statutory citation, and district-level interpretation variance (plus special-education differentiation). The posture is that of a neutral recorder of law: each cell states what the governing authority provides, not whether any jurisdiction's framework is favorable or unfavorable to any party. Each jurisdiction was researched as an independent task by a dedicated research lane and then adversarially validated by a separate lane that re-opened every cited source and challenged every field; a final pass re-opened each jurisdiction's primary statutory citation. No rule from one jurisdiction was used to infer or fill a gap in another's, and no ranking or comparison is made.

## Notes / Special Provisions

### District of Columbia

For the District of Columbia, several provisions affect the practical application of the five fields beyond the core 5-year ceiling. Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions: D.C. Code § 2-354.02 sets the competitive-sealed-bidding threshold at contracts > $100,000; smaller procurements may proceed under § 2-354.07's small-purchase methods, and charter LEAs apply a $25,000 public-bidding threshold under § 38-1802.04(c)(1)(A) and DC PCSB policy. Sole-source / emergency procurement: § 2-354.04 (sole source, CPO D&F + 10-day posted notice of intent) and § 2-354.05 (emergency procurements) operate as exceptions; the sole-source procedure under § 2-354.04 with 27 DCMR §§ 1304.1, 1700, 1701, and 2005.2(b) is the documented mechanism the District has used (see DGS Class D&F DCAM-18-NC-0028) to extend a competitively awarded contract beyond the 5-year base + option ceiling. Emergency procurement under § 2-354.05 was used by DCPS for short-term transportation needs (see GAGA-2026-E-0021). Cooperative-purchasing / piggyback authority: § 2-354.10 authorizes cooperative purchasing through GSA schedules, the D.C. Supply Schedule, and cooperative agreements, which can substitute for a fresh competitive procurement at the end of a contract term. Charter-school rules: § 38-1802.04(c)(1)(A) requires a published RFP notice in the D.C. Register and newspapers of general circulation at least 7 days before award for any procurement contract ≥ $25,000 (with exceptions for emergencies, real-property leases, employment, and charter-designated management contracts); DC PCSB administers this uniformly through the Procurement Contract Submission Policy (eff. July 1, 2019). Special-education differences: D.C. law does not establish a separate procurement / term / rebid regime for special-education transportation; OSSE DOT runs an in-house bus fleet for IEP transportation under post-Petties operational arrangements, and supplements through private-contractor capacity (beginning FY23) procured under the same PPRA / Chapter 3A framework. State-approval requirements: multiyear contracts and any contract > $1M in a 12-month period require Council review (deemed-approval or deemed-disapproval rules in § 1-204.51(b)–(c) and § 2-352.02); the Director of OCP must issue a written D&F to permit a base + option term to exceed 5 years (27 DCMR § 2005.2(a)–(b)).

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** D.C. law does not set a separate term-limit, rebid, procurement, or renewal regime for special-education / IEP-mandated student transportation. The OSSE Division of Student Transportation (OSSE DOT) directly operates the District's special-education transportation under federal court oversight that ended in 2012 (Petties v. District of Columbia, 1:95-cv-00148 (D.D.C.)), and primarily uses government-employee bus operators rather than long-term private route contracts; when OSSE supplements capacity through private transportation contracts (see OSSE DOT Internal Strategic Roadmap FY25-26 expansion of private-contractor capacity beginning FY23), those contracts are procured under the same PPRA / Chapter 3A and 27 DCMR framework as other District contracts (D.C. Code § 2-351.05(a); §§ 2-354.01–.04, 2-355.04; 27 DCMR § 2005). McKinney-Vento and foster-care transportation are administered as services arranged by the LEAs (DCPS and charter LEAs) under their general procurement authorities; no D.C. statute or regulation establishes a different term/rebid/renewal rule for those route types. Federal IDEA, McKinney-Vento, and ESSA obligations create the duty to transport but do not alter the District's contracting / procurement rules.

### South Dakota

South Dakota's student-transportation contracting regime is set by SDCL 5-18C-1 (a flat five-year cap on any single contract for transportation of students) operating in tandem with SDCL 5-18A-22(12) (renumbered to (13) effective July 1, 2026), which expressly exempts a school district's purchase 'for transportation of students' from the competitive-bidding requirements of chapters 5-18A, 5-18B, 5-18C, and 5-18D. The practical effect is that the district must enter into a new contract at the end of the five-year term (because no contract may exceed five years), but state law does not require the re-award to be made through a competitive sealed bid or RFP. If a district nonetheless chooses to use public bidding, SDCL 13-29-20 separately bars the school district itself from participating as a bidder. Within the five-year term, SDCL 5-18C-1 allows the parties to renegotiate 'specific provisions' if the contract contains guidelines for making such changes, and any change must be reported in the school-board minutes; the statute does not authorize CPI/fuel-escalator clauses, evergreen rollover, or state-agency approval mechanisms, so any such mechanic would have to be drafted into the contract itself within the five-year ceiling. The Department of Legislative Audit's 'Bid Booklet' (July 1, 2024) authoritatively states this joint reading on page 6, item (g). Other generally applicable procurement carve-outs (sole-source procurement under SDCL 5-18A-32, emergency procurement under SDCL 5-18A-32 and related provisions, cooperative/piggyback purchasing from another government's competitively awarded contract under SDCL 5-18A-22(3), state-contract-list purchases under SDCL 5-18C-8) are available to school districts as procurement tools, but the SDCL 5-18A-22(12) exemption already removes student-transportation contracts from the bidding regime, so those carve-outs are typically not needed for the procurement itself. Special-education transportation is contracted under the same regime: SDCL 5-18C-1 and SDCL 5-18A-22(12) both speak to 'transportation of students' without distinguishing special education; SDCL 13-37-8.9 addresses how special-education transportation may be paid (parent mileage allowance no less than the state mileage rate per SDCL 3-9-1; in lieu of mileage, common-carrier cost or 'bus service provided by contract with the facility in which the child is enrolled'), but imposes no different contract-term or competitive-bidding rule on the contract itself. No state-agency approval is required for the contract under SDCL 5-18C-1. ARSD 24:05 (special-education rules) addresses transportation as a related service but does not impose a separate contract-term or procurement rule on transportation contracts. The SD DOE School Transportation page (doe.sd.gov/oatq/transportation.aspx) confirms DOE's role is limited to driver training, safety inspections, and licensing (citing SDCL 13-29-21 on driver training every five years), not contract term or procurement. Charter-school procurement is not separately differentiated by SD statute for student-transportation contracts. ASBSD maintains sample policies DJ (Purchasing Procedures) and DJC (Bidding Requirements) consistent with the same statutory framework.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** South Dakota law does NOT set different contract-term limits, rebid requirements, procurement rules, or renewal mechanics for special-education transportation, IEP-mandated transportation, McKinney-Vento transportation, foster-care transportation, or alternative student transportation. SDCL 5-18C-1 (the five-year cap) speaks to 'transportation of students' without distinguishing special education. SDCL 5-18A-22(12) likewise exempts a school district's purchase 'for transportation of students' without distinguishing special education. SDCL 13-37-8.9 (within the special-education chapter, Chapter 13-37) addresses how special-education transportation may be paid - including, in lieu of parent mileage, 'the actual cost of transportation by common carrier or of bus service provided by contract with the facility in which the child is enrolled' - and provides a parent mileage allowance (no less than the state mileage rate under SDCL 3-9-1) when district bus service is not available, but it does not impose a different term limit or procurement regime on the contract itself. Federal IDEA and McKinney-Vento law impose duties to provide transportation but do not, by themselves, alter SD's state-law contract-term or competitive-bidding regime; that is a federal-duty-to-transport overlay, not a SD contract or procurement rule. No SD ARSD provision (including the 24:05 special-education rules) was located setting a special-education-specific contract-term or competitive-bidding rule for transportation contracts.

### Tennessee

Tennessee's transportation-specific authority sits in Tenn. Code Ann. § 49-6-2101(d)-(e), and the general school-procurement statute in Tenn. Code Ann. § 49-2-203(a)(3) reaches only supplies/furniture/fixtures/materials and construction - so the practical procurement framework for school-bus contracts depends on the LEA's chosen procedure under § 49-2-203(a)(3)(A). Under that subsection, an LEA may follow its local governing body's procurement procedure where the charter, private act, or ordinance provides for advertisement and competitive bidding; if the LEA does not adopt the local procedure, then the default school-procurement rule under § 49-2-203(a)(3)(B)(ii) applies, with newspaper-advertised sealed bids required above $10,000 (counties under 40,000 population) or $25,000 (counties 40,000 or more). Under Tenn. Code Ann. § 12-3-1212, those thresholds may be raised by resolution of the governing body to $50,000 (centralized purchasing with full-time agent) or $25,000 (otherwise). Cooperative-purchasing authority under Tenn. Code Ann. § 12-3-1201 permits LEAs and other local entities to purchase under Tennessee Department of General Services Central Procurement Office contracts without separate advertisement and competitive bidding ('buying under state contract'). Emergencies allow waiver of newspaper advertisement (§ 49-2-203(a)(3)(B)(i)). Charter schools are LEAs for procurement purposes; SBE Rule 0520-01-09-.02 includes charter schools in the special-education framework. State approval is involved indirectly through SBE Pupil Transportation Rule 0520-01-05 (minimum bus standards, insurance, bus age) and through state-funded transportation eligibility rules in § 49-6-2101(a)-(b). Special-education transportation is governed by SBE Rule 0520-01-09-.05(e), which authorizes LEA contracting and imposes vehicle, operator-training, contractor-eligibility, and parity standards but does NOT impose separate contract-term, rebid, or renewal rules. The practical effect of these provisions is that the six-year ceiling in § 49-6-2101(e) operates as the only express statutory term limit, while initial term, renewal/extension structure, CPI/index escalation, fuel escalators, and rebid practice are matters of district contract drafting and procurement procedure - with documented inter-district variance ranging from 3-year fixed terms (Hamilton County) to 6 years + up to six 1-year extensions (Anderson County). The latter structure raises an unresolved interpretive question whether the six-year ceiling permits successive option-year extensions, on which no AG opinion or court decision was located.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** No located Tennessee statute, regulation, AG opinion, or State Board of Education rule prescribes different term limits, rebid requirements, procurement rules, or renewal mechanics for special-education transportation, IEP-mandated transportation, McKinney-Vento transportation, foster-care transportation, alternative-student transportation, or vendors serving students with disabilities. Tenn. Code Ann. § 49-6-2101 applies generally to contracting 'for transportation services with persons owning equipment' and contains no carve-out for special-education routes. State Board of Education Rule 0520-01-05-.02(2) provides that 'Vehicles constructed for transporting children with disabilities shall comply generally with the Tennessee Minimum School Bus Standards approved by the State Board of Education, but because of special equipment, appropriate modifications shall be made for children with disabilities' - this is an equipment / modification standard, not a contracting rule. State Board of Education Rule 0520-01-09-.05(e) addresses 'Special Transportation' under Special Education Programs and Services: (1) requires that children with disabilities be provided transportation along with non-disabled children whenever appropriate; (2) travel-time parity (with IEP-team exceptions); (3) vehicles used for special transportation must meet the requirements of the Pupil Transportation Rule 0520-01-05; (4) operators and attendants providing special transportation 'shall be given special training regarding the needs and special requirements of children with disabilities'; and (5) 'LEAs may contract for special transportation provided that the operators, attendants, and vehicles used by a contractor meet the requirements established by the State Board of Education, except when Parents are transporting children with disabilities.' This special-education rule qualifies vehicle and operator standards and confirms LEA contracting authority but does NOT impose separate contract-term, rebid, or renewal rules. Federal IDEA and McKinney-Vento create state duties to transport but do not impose state contract-term, rebid, or renewal rules. CTAS guidance treats special-education transportation as covered by the same LEA-procurement framework, and verified district practice (e.g., Warren County RFP 2026) bundles 'special needs routes' inside the same RFP and contract as regular routes. Negative search: no separate IEP-, McKinney-Vento-, foster-care-, or alternative-student-specific contracting statute located in Title 49 Chapter 6 Part 21 (sections 49-6-2101 through 49-6-2119), and none located in Title 49 Chapter 10 (Special Education) affecting contract term, rebid, or renewal rules.

### Texas

Texas school district transportation contracting sits at the intersection of three statutes: Tex. Educ. Code Sec. 34.008 (services contracts with a mass transit authority, commercial transportation company, or juvenile board); Sec. 34.009 (contracts for the use, acquisition, or lease with option to purchase of a school bus); and Sec. 44.031 (the general school district procurement statute). Sec. 34.008 services contracts have no statutory term cap. Sec. 34.009 bus-use/acquisition/lease contracts must be between 2 and 10 years. The general procurement framework requires use of one of seven best-value methods (competitive bidding, competitive sealed proposals, RFP, interlocal contract, Ch. 2269 methods for construction, reverse auction, or political-subdivision-corporation formation) for school district services contracts valued at $100,000 or more per 12-month period (threshold raised from $50,000 by S.B. 1173, 89R, eff. 9/1/2025; transition rule limits new threshold to purchases on or after the effective date). For school bus purchase or lease, Sec. 44.031(l) imposes a separate $20,000 competitive bidding threshold. Interlocal contracts under Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 791 (cross-referenced in Sec. 34.007(a)(2)) are an authorized best-value method and are commonly used for cooperative purchasing/piggyback structures. Sec. 34.009(c) treats a contract that reserves the board's continuing right to terminate at the expiration of each budget period as a commitment of current revenues only (annual-appropriation/non-substitution structure); Sec. 34.009(d) prohibits termination penalties in Sec. 34.009 contracts. No Texas statute imposes a separate procurement framework for special-education transportation, IEP-mandated transportation, McKinney-Vento, or foster-care transportation contracts; the federal duty to transport SPED students (IDEA, 19 Tex. Admin. Code Ch. 89) does not change Texas contracting law. The TEA School Transportation Allotment Handbook (2023-2024) Section 13 governs administrative implementation but cites only Sec. 34.007 and Sec. 34.008 - it imposes no TEA-level term cap, rebid cycle, or SPED-specific contracting layer. No state-agency approval is required to enter a Sec. 34.008 services contract beyond the general Sec. 44.031 best-value determination. Texas charter schools are LEAs and subject to their own governing instruments; this analysis focuses on traditional school districts. As a matter of district practice (recorded as practice, not statute), Texas district transportation RFPs commonly use a 5-year base term plus a 5-year renewal option exercised by mutual written agreement and board approval; these are contractual and board-policy structures, not statutory.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** Texas state law does not set different contract-term, rebid, renewal, or procurement rules for special-education transportation, IEP-mandated transportation, McKinney-Vento transportation, or foster-care transportation contracts. Tex. Educ. Code Sec. 34.008 (commercial transportation services), Sec. 34.009 (bus use/lease), and Sec. 44.031 (school district purchasing) make no distinction by student population served. The DUTY to provide special-education transportation is governed by IDEA (federal) and Texas's special-education rules (e.g., 19 Tex. Admin. Code Ch. 89), and may be a related service identified by the ARD/IEP committee - but the procurement framework for the underlying transportation services contract follows the general school-district contracting rules. The TEA School Transportation Allotment Handbook (2023-2024) Section 13 governs contracting of transportation services and cites only Tex. Educ. Code Sec. 34.007 and Sec. 34.008 - no SPED-specific contracting carve-out. Where a parent or designated agent provides transportation under an IEP, TEA recommends a written contract stipulating reimbursement terms and proof of liability insurance, but again does not impose a different term/rebid framework.

### Utah

Several Utah Procurement Code provisions and related rules materially shape the practical application of the five fields for student-transportation contracts: (1) Small-purchase / dollar-threshold rules — under R277-122 and § 63G-6a-506, Utah procurement units (including school districts) may use a small-purchase process below statutory thresholds; an individual school district may set lower internal small-purchase limits than the State allows (the State Auditor cites an example of an entity using $350 instead of $1,000), but cannot exceed statutory ceilings. (2) Sole source / emergency procurement — § 63G-6a-802 authorizes procurement without a standard procurement process in cases of sole source (Subsection (1)(a)) or emergency, and is the principal statutory route for a school district to avoid an otherwise required rebid at term-end. (3) Cooperative purchasing / piggyback authority — § 63G-6a-2105 permits a procurement unit (including a school district) to participate in a cooperative procurement with other Utah procurement units or public entities, subject to participating-addendum and notice conditions; this can substitute for a standalone competitive procurement at term-end. (4) Approved vendor list — § 63G-6a-507 (referenced in § 63G-6a-103(3)) authorizes use of an approved-vendor-list process. (5) Limited extension without procurement — § 63G-6a-802.7 allows a procurement official to extend an existing contract without a standard procurement process for ≤120 days to avoid a lapse in a critical government service or for the period of a protest/appeal, and for longer than 120 days only after consulting the attorney general or unit's attorney and making a written determination. (6) Annual-appropriation contingency — § 63G-6a-1204(3) makes any multiyear contract conditional on continued appropriations after year one (with parallel federal-funds rules in subsections (4)–(5)). (7) Charter-school rules — charter schools are explicitly within the Procurement Code as "educational procurement units" (§ 63G-6a-103(30)(b)) and the State Auditor's brief confirms charter schools are subject to the Code. (8) Special-education differentiation — none under Utah law; R277-600-5 governs service-delivery and reimbursement for students with disabilities but does not modify procurement contract mechanics, so § 63G-6a-1204's 5-year cap, rebid mandate, and conditional-renewal rules apply identically to IEP-mandated, McKinney-Vento, foster-care, and alternative student-transportation contracts. (9) State-approval requirements — USBE's R277-600 requires Superintendent approval of bus routes and reimbursement (operational, not procurement-contract-term). (10) Title 41 safety overlay — § 41-6a-1304 and Title 41 broadly give UDOT safety-regulation authority over school buses operated by or under contract with a school district, independent of and unaffected by the Procurement Code.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** Utah law does not impose different contract-term limits, rebid requirements, procurement rules, or renewal mechanics for special-education / IEP-mandated student transportation. Utah Admin. Code R277-600-5 (Student with Disabilities Transportation) addresses service-delivery and reimbursement: a student with a disability shall be transported on regular buses and regular routes when possible, unless the IEP team determines otherwise; a school district may be reimbursed for the costs of transporting or for alternative transportation for students with disabilities whose severity of disability, or combination of disabilities, necessitates special transportation; and minimum-duration thresholds (15 days during the regular school year / 10 days during ESY) apply to qualifying for reimbursement. The rule does not modify the Utah Procurement Code's contract-term, rebid, or renewal rules. IDEA (20 U.S.C. § 1400 et seq.) and 34 C.F.R. § 300.34(c)(16) require transportation as a related service when the IEP team determines it is needed, but that federal obligation goes to the duty to transport, not to state contracting/procurement rules. The same Utah Code § 63G-6a-1204 framework (5-year cap, rebid at term-end, written-determination exception, conditional-renewal mechanics) therefore applies to special-education / alternative student-transportation contracts. McKinney-Vento and foster-care transportation duties are similarly federally anchored on the duty side and do not alter Utah procurement rules.

### Vermont

Vermont school-district transportation contracting is controlled by a single statewide public-bid statute (16 V.S.A. § 559) that interlocks with several practical carve-outs. Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemption: § 559(a) is triggered only when cost exceeds $40,000; transportation contracts below that threshold do not require the formal three-bid process. Emergency procurement: § 559(e)(3) exempts emergency repairs entirely. Cooperative purchasing / piggyback: § 559(e)(5) permits supervisory unions and school districts to enter into joint-bidding agreements consistent with § 559; § 559(e)(6) permits purchases through the Commissioner of Buildings and General Services under 29 V.S.A. ch. 49. Board waiver / state-agency approval: § 559(f) permits the Secretary of Education to grant individual waivers where the board shows it cannot comply through no fault of its own and proposes a fair and public alternative method. Charter-school rules: Vermont charter authorization is narrow and the Stage 2 record contains no charter-specific procurement carve-out from § 559. Special-education differences: none — Rule Series 2360 imposes a duty to provide transportation as a related service, but no separate procurement / contract-term regime applies to IEP, McKinney-Vento, or alternative-student transportation contracts procured by districts (federal 2 CFR § 200.320(c)(4) sole-source prior-approval applies only to federally funded procurement). Consequence of violation: any contract entered into in violation of § 559 carries the risk of denial of state aid under § 559(g). The renewal mechanism in § 559(e)(7) is the single statewide rebid-avoidance lever for ordinary transportation contracts — its NEEP-CPI annual cap and 30% cumulative-price cap operate as practical limits on how long a renewal chain can run before a fresh § 559(a) competitive process is required.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** Vermont State Board of Education Special Education Rule Series 2360-2369 (effective July 1, 2022) defines 'related services' to include 'transportation' (Rule 2360.2.16(a)) and details that 'Transportation includes: (i) Travel to and from school and between schools; (ii) Travel in and around school buildings; and (iii) Specialized equipment (such as special or adapted buses, lifts, and ramps), if required to provide special transportation for a child with a disability' (Rule 2360.2.16(d)(15)). These rules govern the DUTY to provide transportation as a related service under an IEP — they impose no separate or differentiated procurement, contract-term, rebid, or renewal regime for special-education transportation contracts with private contractors. Vermont's school-district public-bid statute (16 V.S.A. § 559) and its $40,000 threshold and renewal-without-rebid conditions therefore apply equally to special-education / IEP-mandated / McKinney-Vento transportation procured by school districts or supervisory unions. Federal IDEA (20 U.S.C. § 1401(26)) and the EDGAR/2 CFR Part 200 uniform-guidance procurement rules also apply when federal funds are used, but they govern federal-funds procurement standards generally and do not impose state-law contract-term limits. The Vermont AOE federal-fiscal services page confirms that sole-source (non-competitive) procurement of federally funded services requires prior AOE approval per 2 CFR § 200.320(c)(4), but this is a federal-funds procedural overlay, not a state contract-term rule.

### Virginia

Practical-application notes for Virginia, all anchored to official authority: (i) Small-purchase thresholds — Va. Code § 2.2-4303(G)–(H) permits small-purchase procedures below specified thresholds ($80,000–$300,000 depending on category) provided written procedures are adopted; competition must still be pursued 'wherever practicable.' (ii) Sole-source and emergency procurement — Va. Code § 2.2-4303(F)–(G) permit non-competitive procurement upon written determination of sole-source justification or emergency, with such competition 'as is practicable under the circumstances.' (iii) Cooperative purchasing / piggyback — Va. Code § 2.2-4304 expressly authorizes joint and cooperative procurement; school divisions routinely satisfy procurement obligations by piggybacking eligible cooperative contracts in lieu of an independent solicitation. (iv) School-division alternative policies — Va. Code § 2.2-4343(A)(11) permits a school board to adopt 'alternative policies and procedures' exempting it from particular VPPA articles only if such policies are 'based on competitive principles and generally applicable'; § 2.2-4343(A)(12) requires continued compliance with enumerated VPPA sections (including subsections B, C, and D of § 2.2-4303, professional-services competitive negotiation when cost exceeds $80,000, and §§ 2.2-4305, 2.2-4311, 2.2-4315, 2.2-4317, 2.2-4330, 2.2-4333 through 2.2-4338, 2.2-4342, 2.2-4343.1, 2.2-4367 through 2.2-4377). (v) Public hearing before transferring noninstructional services — Va. Code § 22.1-79(8) requires a public hearing (with at least seven days' newspaper notice) before a school board transfers administration of all noninstructional services in the division to a private contractor; this requirement attaches to the initial transfer decision and is not waivable by solicitation drafting. (vi) Contract-modification cap — Va. Code § 2.2-4309(A) caps fixed-price modifications at 25% or $50,000 (whichever is greater) absent governing-body approval, providing a practical ceiling on mid-term price increases. (vii) Charter schools — Virginia public charter schools operate under the authority of and within local school divisions (Va. Code §§ 22.1-212.5 et seq.), so the same VPPA rules and school-board policies apply to their transportation procurement. (viii) Special-education transportation — no separate Virginia statute or 8VAC20-70 rule sets distinct procurement procedures; federal IDEA and McKinney-Vento obligations affect the duty to transport but do not displace the state procurement framework. (ix) State-approval requirement — there is no statewide state-agency pre-approval mandate for school-division pupil-transportation contract awards; VDOE establishes minimum school-bus specifications and oversees DGS competitive bid processes for the statewide bus-purchase contract, but routine division-level service-contract awards are not subject to VDOE pre-approval.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** No separate Virginia statute or Board of Education regulation sets different term limits, rebid requirements, procurement rules, or renewal mechanics for special-education transportation, IEP-mandated transportation, McKinney-Vento transportation, foster-care transportation, or vendors serving students with disabilities. Va. Code Title 22.1, Ch. 12 (Pupil Transportation) and 8VAC20-70 do not differentiate procurement rules by student population, and the VPPA does not contain a special-education carve-out for contracting procedures. Federal IDEA and McKinney-Vento obligations affect the duty to provide transportation but, on Virginia-law research as of the access date, do not change the state contract-term or procurement rules. Not located: any Virginia AG opinion or VDOE guidance setting a distinct procurement track for special-education transportation contracting.

### Washington

Several Washington-specific provisions affect practical application of the five fields. (1) Small-purchase thresholds: RCW 28A.335.190 sets a $40,000 written/telephonic-quotation floor (requiring at least three sources) and a >$75,000 formal-bidding threshold; those thresholds apply only when a district chooses the RCW 28A.160.140(4)(b)(i) bid-law track of the open competitive process. (2) Sole-source/emergency procurement: RCW 28A.335.190 includes board waivers when delay would cause material injury to the district or its property (emergency exception), but no provision in RCW 28A.160.140 waives the 5-year (or 7-year zero-emission) open-competitive-process trigger itself. (3) Cooperative purchasing/piggyback: not addressed by RCW 28A.160.140; districts may still use interlocal cooperative arrangements under Washington's general interlocal cooperation framework, but a private-contractor pupil transportation services contract entered into directly by a district remains subject to the 5/7-year open-competitive-process trigger. (4) Charter-school rules: Washington charter public schools are LEAs operating under RCW 28A.710 and are not covered by the school-district statutory references in RCW 28A.160.140 in the same way; the statute by its terms applies to 'school district' contracts. (5) Special-education differences: none in the contract-term/rebid/renewal regime; WAC 392-172A-02095 governs delivery modes for IEP-mandated transportation, and WAC chapter 392-141 defines 'private party contract' and 'in lieu transportation' as funding/mode mechanisms. (6) State-approval requirements: contracts entered into, renewed, or extended after June 6, 2024 must satisfy the SEBB-equivalent health-benefits and SERS-equivalent pension conditions in RCW 28A.160.140(2) for opt-in employees, include the detailed cost-increase explanation, and (post-cost-parity, per (3) and RCW 28A.160.260) use zero-emission school buses unless an OSPI exception under RCW 28A.160.260(3)(a)(ii)-(iv) applies. (7) Direct bus acquisition: RCW 28A.160.195 exempts direct school-bus purchases from the general school-district bid law, but does not displace the open-competitive-process trigger for services contracts under RCW 28A.160.140.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** RCW 28A.160.140 applies to "pupil transportation services contract[s]," defined in subsection (4)(c) as contracts for the operation of privately owned or school-district-owned school buses and the services of drivers, operators, supervisors, and support personnel "to provide students with transportation to and from school on a regular basis." The definition is not restricted to regular-education routes; no separate Washington statute was located imposing different maximum-term, rebid, or renewal rules for special education, IEP-mandated, McKinney-Vento, foster-care, or alternative student transportation contracts. WAC 392-172A-02095 (Provision of Special Education Services - Transportation) sequences delivery modes for IEP-mandated transportation (school-bus transportation; private-party contracts; in-lieu/parent reimbursement; board-and-room when other options are infeasible) and requires that vehicles and operations comply with chapters 392-143, 392-144, and 392-145 WAC, but does not impose a separate contract-term, rebid, or renewal rule that displaces RCW 28A.160.140. WAC chapter 392-141 likewise defines "private party contract" and "in lieu transportation" as funding/mode arrangements without imposing different contract-term or rebid rules. Federal IDEA establishes the duty to provide transportation as a related service when written into the IEP but does not displace state contract/procurement rules.

### West Virginia

West Virginia's framework for student-transportation contracts is governed almost entirely by W. Va. Board of Education legislative rule Policy 8200 (W. Va. C.S.R. §126-202), implemented under the county-board transportation authority of W. Va. Code §18-5-13. Several discretionary mechanisms within Policy 8200 affect the practical operation of the five fields: small-purchase / dollar-threshold treatment is structured around §7.11's tiered bid thresholds (verbal quotes at $5,000, written bids at $10,000, advertised bids at $25,000, sealed advertised bids at $50,000), with LEAs free to lower the thresholds further by local policy; sole-source / direct-purchase authority is recognized under §3.2.5.a(6) and the §8.1 alternative-procurement procedures (which also cover situations where no bidders respond); cooperative-purchasing / piggyback authority is recognized for State open-ended contracts (§§3.2.5.a.c, 8.1.4), GSA schedules (§8.1.5), RESA and inter-county cooperatives (§8.1.7), and national governmental purchasing cooperatives such as AEPA, U.S. Communities, and TCPN (§8.1.8); §9 exempts an enumerated list of services from competitive bid (including the annual audit and certain professional services). The WV Code provides a separate, equipment-only framework for state contracts on school buses through §5A-3-11, which is administered through Policy 8200 §19.1.1 and which does not reach transportation services contracts. Charter schools are addressed in W. Va. Code §18-5G-5, which adjusts transportation funding allocations but does not establish a different procurement framework for charter-school transportation contracts. Special-education student transportation is required under §18-20-2 and is operationalized through Policy 2419 (126 CSR 16) and Policy 4336 (126 CSR 92) — including IEP/§504-specified bus modifications — but no separate procurement, term, or renewal rule applies; Policy 4336 §17.5's 'contract operators' framework governs individual operator qualifications (background checks, training, $1,000,000 auto-liability minimum) in limited-service areas rather than the LEA's procurement of a transportation services contract. No state-agency approval is required to renew a county-board transportation contract; renewals require only mutual written consent of the LEA and vendor, a written letter of justification on file, and compliance with the two-successive-one-year cap. Policy 8200 expressly contemplates that LEAs will adopt local supplementing policies that may make purchasing requirements more restrictive but may not relax the state-board floor.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** W. Va. Code §18-20-2(a) lists 'transportation' as a special service the county board 'shall provide' to implement each student's IEP, and §18-20-2(b) authorizes a county board to 'provide for educating resident exceptional children by contracting with other counties or other educational agencies which maintain special education facilities' — an inter-LEA contracting authority, not a private-vendor procurement rule. Neither §18-20-2, nor W. Va. C.S.R. §126-92 (Policy 4336, including §17.5 governing 'contract operators' in limited-service areas, which sets background-check, training, and $1,000,000 auto-liability requirements but no term/renewal/procurement rule), nor W. Va. C.S.R. §126-202 (Policy 8200) establishes a different maximum contract term, different rebidding standard, or different renewal mechanism for special-education, IEP-mandated, McKinney-Vento, foster-care, alternative, or disability-student transportation than for regular-route transportation. Policy 4373 (LEA student conduct/safety) and Policy 2419 / 126 CSR 16 (Regulations for the Education of Students with Exceptionalities) address bus modifications and supports specified in IEPs and §504 plans, but they go to the duty to transport and operational accommodations rather than to contract term, renewal, or procurement. Federal IDEA obligations attach to the duty to transport but do not displace West Virginia's state contract and procurement rules.

### Wisconsin

Wisconsin imposes no statewide statutory maximum term, no rebid requirement, and no renewal mechanics on a public school district's contract with a private student-transportation contractor under Wis. Stat. 121.55(1)(a). Practical considerations for the five fields include: (i) Wis. Stat. 121.52(2)(b)-(c) requires that any contract with a private vehicle owner or lessee transporting pupils for compensation be in writing on a DPI-prescribed form; (ii) Wis. Stat. 66.0901 (general municipal public-contracts bidding statute) includes school districts in the definition of 'municipality' but reaches only 'public contracts' for construction or supplies/material 'required to be advertised for by law' - school district transportation service procurement is not within that scope, so no statewide bidding mandate is imposed; (iii) Wis. Stat. 120.12(24)(a) is the only sealed-bid mandate in the school-board-duties statute and applies solely to group health-care benefits, not transportation; (iv) Wis. Stat. 16.73(1) and Wis. Stat. 66.0301(2) authorize school districts to engage in cooperative purchasing / cooperative service arrangements with other municipalities (permissive, not mandatory, and not a term limit); (v) Wis. Admin. Code ch. PI 7 sets minimum compensation rules for parent-guardian and certain private-school transportation contracts (PI 7.04, PI 7.05) - those rules do not apply to common-carrier contracts under 121.55(1)(a); (vi) charter schools chartered under Wisconsin law operate under a charter contract with the sponsoring school board or other chartering authority and are largely freed from state procurement rules; transportation arrangements remain governed by the chartering authority's own contracting practices; (vii) special-education transportation is governed by IDEA-aligned ch. 115 subch. V and DPI Bulletin 18.01, neither of which imposes contracting rules different from regular routes; (viii) DPI does not require state-agency approval of common-carrier transportation contracts (state-superintendent approval is required only for the duty to transport children with disabilities regardless of distance under 121.54(3)); and (ix) district policies vary materially in practice - Appleton Area School District Policy 672 caps service contracts (including pupil transportation) at five years with 1-year renewals, while Milwaukee Public Schools uses a formal RFP framework.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** Wisconsin's special-education statutes (Wis. Stat. ch. 115, subch. V, incorporating IDEA Part B at 20 U.S.C. 1400-1482 via Wis. Stat. 115.758) and DPI Information Update Bulletin 18.01 require districts to provide transportation as a related service when the IEP team determines it is necessary, and address logistics (pick-up/drop-off, parent reimbursement, safety) - but they do not impose different contract-term limits, rebid requirements, renewal mechanics, or procurement rules for special-education transportation than for regular routes. The contracting authority remains Wis. Stat. 121.55(1) for both, and IDEA federal law speaks to the duty to transport, not to state contract/procurement rules. McKinney-Vento and foster-care transportation duties (federal) likewise do not impose Wisconsin-law contracting rules. Districts (notably MPS RFP 1147) do procure special-education transportation through separate RFPs as a matter of practice, but that practice is not state-law-mandated.

### Wyoming

Wyoming's transportation-contract framework is materially shaped by what it does not address. The operative school-district procurement statute, W.S. § 21-3-110(a)(viii), establishes only dollar-threshold-driven competitive bidding for 'insurance, supplies or materials' and a separate publication regime for capital-construction contracts; it sets no transportation-services-specific contract-term cap, no rebid requirement tied to the end of a transportation-services term, and no renewal mechanics. Small-purchase / dollar-threshold exemptions: the statute treats purchases below $25,000 as outside the bidding requirement and purchases between $25,000 and $50,000 as triggering competitive bids without the formal publication regime; at or above $50,000 the call for bids must be published in a newspaper of general circulation and on the state procurement website (as defined in W.S. § 9-2-3001(b)(vii)), and the contract may not be divided to avoid the requirement. Sole-source / emergency procurement: Wyoming statutes do not provide a unified sole-source / emergency-procurement framework for school-district services contracts (in contrast to the state-agency framework that operates around W.S. § 9-2-3001 et seq.); the 2025 LSO cooperative-purchasing memo confirms there is no express statutory authority for districts to use cooperative-purchasing / piggyback procurement and proposes legislative amendments to authorize it. Charter-school rules: charter-school transportation arrangements remain subject to general district transportation responsibilities under W.S. § 21-13-320 and to district policy; no separate transportation-contract term, rebid, or renewal rule for charter schools was located. Special-education differences: none in transportation contracting — W.S. §§ 21-2-501 / 21-2-502 entitle children with disabilities to FAPE and authorize a district unable to provide programs / services to 'contract with another school district or agency' to obtain them; W.S. § 21-13-321 funds special-education programs and services at one hundred percent (100%) of the prior year's actual expenditure; neither establishes a separate procurement, term, or renewal rule for special-education transportation contracts. State-approval requirements: WDE approval applies to vehicle purchases for reimbursement (Chapter 20 and W.S. § 21-13-320(f)–(g)) and to the State Vehicle Bid Process at the WDE / Department of Administration & Information level, but does not extend to approval of district contracts with private transportation contractors. Practical application of the five fields: because the operative statute is silent on term, rebid at term-end, and renewal mechanics for transportation services, individual districts make these calls under their own purchasing policies, which mirror the 21-3-110(a)(viii) dollar thresholds but vary in their treatment of renewals and extensions (CCSD #1 exempts renewals/extensions of maintenance/service agreements under $25,000 from board approval; NCSD #1 caps online subscriptions/license agreements at 12 months absent specified termination clauses, but does not impose a transportation-services term cap). Statutory interpretive question: the BOCES clause in 21-3-110(a)(viii) refers to 'goods or services that are subject to this paragraph,' which raises an unresolved textual question about whether the bidding requirement reaches services (including transportation services) more broadly than 'insurance, supplies or materials'; no Wyoming AG opinion or court decision resolves the question.

**Special-education differentiation:** Different rules? **No.** No separate Wyoming statute or WDE administrative rule sets different contract term limits, rebid requirements, procurement rules, or renewal mechanics for special-education transportation, IEP-mandated transportation, McKinney-Vento transportation, foster-care transportation, alternative student transportation, or vendors serving students with disabilities. W.S. § 21-2-501 entitles 'Every child of school age in the state of Wyoming having a mental, physical or psychological disability which impairs learning ... to a free and appropriate education,' and W.S. § 21-2-502 provides that '[i]f the school district is unable to provide the necessary and appropriate programs and services, it shall contract with another school district or agency to obtain them' — a general inter-agency contracting authorization, not a procurement-rules carve-out. (The Stage 1 pack attributed this 'shall contract' language to '21-2-501 et seq.'; the specific quote sits in 21-2-502, which is part of the 21-2-501 et seq. article — verified.) W.S. § 21-13-321 governs state reimbursement of special-education programs and services (which may include related transportation) at one hundred percent (100%) of the amount actually expended in the prior year, but does not establish separate procurement, term, or rebid rules. Federal IDEA (20 U.S.C. § 1400 et seq.) establishes the duty to provide transportation as a related service under an IEP; it does not modify Wyoming contract or procurement rules. WDE Chapter 20 (the student-transportation reimbursement rule) does not differentiate procurement, term, or renewal mechanics for special-education transportation.
